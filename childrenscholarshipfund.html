Children's Scholarship Fund

UX\UI Case Study

How can we address the needs of the users with visual impairments to help the organisation achieve its business objectives?

Overview

Timeline

July 2022, 4 weeks

Team

Zacharry Faihz, Nekha Radha Das, Sydneigh Vitlen

My role

User Research, Prototyping, UX Design, UI Design, Landing Pages, Design System

Background

My team and I wanted to contribute to the prosperity of a non-profit we care deeply about, which led us choosing a non-profit that shares our values and cares about the future generations. Children’s Scholarship Fund is an NYC non-profit that cuts out the middleman and addresses academic needs of low-income families.

Aim

To help to achieve business objectives by providing a better user experience for the users with special needs.

Success rate of Our Attitudinal UX-KPIs


Problem\Solution

The Problem

The challenge is to improve the experience for users with special needs who might be having trouble navigating the website and make it easier for the organisation to achieve its business objective

The Potential Solution

We have a potential to increase our key audience and thus the revenue. It lies in addressing accessibility issues. We have to reframe our MVP and prioritise our content, so that the user receives all the needed information about our mission, support , feels valued and decides to support us.

Research


“ The disability dollar should be as important to all of us as any other dollar. “
- Kevin Bradley, McDonald's director of inclusion and diversity

To understand the complexity of the problem we needed to conduct a white paper research to see how a business can reap unexpected benefits from catering to people with special needs.
McDonald’s has received high profits from its marketing efforts. When they first featured a para-athlete on their bag, consumers started calling their 800 number to thank them for the inclusion. How did our research shape our design ideas?

Ideation

User Flow

Who is our user persona? After the research step, we needed to identify the target audience. Meet Teresa, our user persona. She is a bilingual mobile phone user with low vision.


As we were moving on with our project , we decided to keep in mind our hypothesis statement for our design:
How Might We help users with disabilities donate and feel valued for their contributions to the non-profit?


Next, we wanted to explore what non-profit sites are currently successful and what makes them successful. As such, we conducted a competitor analysis to see how their advantages could be integrated into our project.


Meet our user-persona: Teresa Ortega!

Although we saw from our SWOT table that a seamless user journey was important, we wanted to hear firsthand our target audience’s experiences to cater the final product towards their needs.

“ I wish the homepage had no visual noise and a straightforward donation process. “
- Participant A

Our interviewees reported a positive user experience when the website had support features for users with special needs. E.g. Colour Blind Safe Palette, Chatbot, Format Size and Zoom In.

With these insights we organised our data through an affinity map. We took direct quotes from our participants and added them to the map.

“ It took me 2 minutes to locate the chatbot, and I had to zoom in to read the text in the nav bar. “
- Participant B

User testing results

Our participants shared with us their concern about our Lo-Wi and Mid -Fi prototypes design. We concluded that before we started designing the Hi-Fi prototype, we needed to update the design according to the feedback.

Wireframes


As we were moving forward to design we considered different design solutions to promote usability and accessibility and inclusion. These aspects are closely related in creating a web or an app that works for everyone. To touch upon a style guide we created we took into consideration the colour psychology. Because blue is favored by so many people, it is often viewed as a non-threatening colour that can seem conservative and traditional. Blue is often used to decorate offices because research has shown that people are more productive in blue rooms. And this the colour blue became our primary colour. Our accent colour –orange , is a social colour, making people open up and enhance their communication. It is an inviting colour. We looked at our competitors to see how they formatted their UI. It was a turning point in our UI design stage, as it led us to creating many colour palettes and logos. Finally, we decided to remove a gradient and switch to a flat UI design to better fit WCAG standards.

Deliverables


Lastly, our hi-fi prototypes deliver a one-stop donation accompanied with accessibility and usability features E.g. Zoom in, Format Size, Chatbot. As we have mentioned before in our white paper research every person with a disability has a friend, a loved one, so if they are thinking positively about Children’s Scholarship Fund because of a support service feature, that’s probably affecting five or 10 other potential users. Finally, the disability dollar should be as important to all of us as any other dollar.

Takeaways and what we’d do differently with Children’s Scholarship Fund:

Finally, what would we do differently? While working on this project and going through iteration cycles we understood the importance of thinking intentionally about every element of the design and making our target users’ needs a priority at every stage. Working fully through this project taught us the importance of trying to think intentionally about every element of the project and how it can contribute to the end result.
